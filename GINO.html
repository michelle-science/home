Guided Science Notes

Matter

  • Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space.
  • It exists in different states: solid, liquid, gas, and plasma.
  • All physical objects are made of matter.

Mass

  • Mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object.
  • It is usually measured in grams or kilograms.
  • Mass is different from weight, which depends on gravity.

Chemical Energy

  • Chemical energy is stored in the bonds of atoms and molecules.
  • It is released during chemical reactions, like when food is digested or fuel is burned.
  • Batteries and food are examples of things that contain chemical energy.

Thermal Energy

  • Thermal energy is the energy that comes from heat.
  • It is caused by the movement of particles within matter.
  • More movement means higher temperature and more thermal energy.

Physical Energy

  • Physical energy usually refers to mechanical energy, which is the energy of motion and position.
  • Examples include a moving car (kinetic energy) or a stretched rubber band (potential energy).
  • Physical energy is involved whenever something moves or changes position.

Stars

  • Stars are giant balls of hot, glowing gas, mostly hydrogen and helium.
  • They produce energy through nuclear fusion, turning hydrogen into helium.
  • The Sun is the closest star to Earth.

Galaxies

  • Galaxies are huge collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity.
  • Our solar system is in the Milky Way galaxy.
  • There are billions of galaxies in the universe.

Atmosphere

  • The atmosphere is the layer of gases that surrounds a planet, like Earth.
  • It protects us from harmful radiation and helps regulate temperature.
  • Earth's atmosphere is made mostly of nitrogen and oxygen.

Orbit

  • An orbit is the path an object takes as it moves around another object in space, usually because of gravity.
  • Planets orbit the Sun, and moons orbit planets.
  • Orbits can be circular or elliptical (oval-shaped).

Biomes

  • Biomes are large areas of the world with similar climates, plants, and animals.
  • Examples include deserts, forests, grasslands, and tundra.
  • Each biome has its own unique ecosystem.

Primary Succession

  • Primary succession is the process by which life begins to grow in a place where there was no soil before, like after a volcanic eruption.
  • Lichens and mosses are often the first organisms to appear.
  • Over time, soil forms and more complex plants can grow.

Secondary Succession

  • Secondary succession happens in areas where a disturbance (like fire or farming) has destroyed a community but left the soil intact.
  • Plants and animals return more quickly than in primary succession.
  • This process helps ecosystems recover after damage.
